# Marketing Frameworks Cheatsheet

Quick-reference guide for the marketing agent's domain expertise canon. Use this to select the right framework for each artifact type and context.

---

## Framework Selection Matrix

| Artifact Type | Primary Framework | Supporting Frameworks |
|--------------|------------------|----------------------|
| Campaign Brief | AARRR (identify funnel stage) | AIDA/PAS (copy structure), Message-Market Fit Testing |
| Content Pack | Content-Market Fit | AIDA (aware audiences), PAS (unaware audiences) |
| Nurture Sequence | Hook Model | AARRR (activation focus), AIDA (per-email structure) |
| Positioning Document | April Dunford | JTBD (value framing), Category Design (if new category) |
| Messaging Matrix | JTBD | April Dunford (differentiation), PAS (pain-first personas) |
| Competitive Narrative | April Dunford | Category Design (reframe the category) |
| Launch Plan | Category Design (Tier 1), AARRR (Tier 2-3) | StoryBrand (narrative), Full-Funnel Attribution (measurement) |
| Analyst Brief | Category Design | April Dunford (positioning for analysts) |
| Community Strategy | Content-Market Fit | Hook Model (engagement loops), AARRR (referral stage) |

## Framework Details

### 1. Positioning (April Dunford)

**Source**: "Obviously Awesome" by April Dunford

**The sequence matters. Do not skip steps.**

```
Step 1: Competitive Alternatives
  -> What would customers do if your product did not exist?
  -> Not "other products in your category" -- the actual alternatives
     including manual processes, spreadsheets, hiring, doing nothing

Step 2: Unique Attributes
  -> What can you do that alternatives cannot?
  -> Must be provable, not aspirational
  -> Each attribute must pass: "Can a competitor credibly claim this too?"
     If yes, it is not unique.

Step 3: Value
  -> Map each unique attribute to a customer outcome
  -> Feature: "AI-powered code review"
  -> Value: "Ship 40% faster because review bottleneck is eliminated"
  -> The value is always about the customer, never about the product

Step 4: Best-Fit Customers
  -> Who cares most about the value you deliver?
  -> Specific enough to find on LinkedIn
  -> Include: triggering event, current workaround, disqualifying signals

Step 5: Market Category
  -> The context that makes your value obvious
  -> Three options:
     - Existing category (compete head-on)
     - Adjacent category (borrow context, add twist)
     - New category (create and own -- highest risk, highest reward)
```

**When to use**: Every positioning document. Also use Steps 1-3 when crafting differentiation pillars in any campaign brief.

**Common mistake**: Starting at Step 5 (choosing a category) instead of Step 1 (understanding alternatives). The category should emerge from the positioning work, not precede it.

### 2. StoryBrand (Donald Miller)

**Source**: "Building a StoryBrand" by Donald Miller

```
1. Character    -> The customer (not your company)
2. Problem      -> External (surface), Internal (emotional), Philosophical (moral)
3. Guide        -> Your brand positions as the guide (empathy + authority)
4. Plan         -> Simple steps the customer takes (3 steps max)
5. Call to Action -> Direct ("Buy Now") and Transitional ("Download Guide")
6. Success      -> What life looks like after (specific, vivid)
7. Failure      -> What happens if they do not act (real stakes)
```

**When to use**: Landing pages, brand narratives, founder storytelling, webinar scripts, long-form content.

**Key rule**: The customer is always the hero. Your product is the guide. If your messaging puts your product at the center of the story, you have failed.

**Common mistake**: Skipping the "Problem" breakdown. The external problem is obvious. The internal problem (how it makes them feel) and philosophical problem (why it is wrong) are what create emotional resonance.

### 3. AIDA (Attention -> Interest -> Desire -> Action)

```
Attention  -> Pattern interrupt. First line or headline.
             "70% of a PM's week shouldn't be spent writing docs."
             Must earn the next sentence.

Interest   -> Relevance. Show you understand their world.
             "We just shipped AI Copilot -- and it's changing
              how product teams work."
             Bridge from attention to "tell me more."

Desire     -> Proof and vision. Show results, paint the outcome.
             "Beta users cut PRD creation from 3 hours to 12 minutes."
             Make them want what you have.

Action     -> Clear, single next step. No ambiguity.
             "Start your free trial" not "Learn more."
             Reduce friction to zero.
```

**When to use**: Ad copy, email bodies, landing page sections, social posts for audiences that already know they have the problem (aware audiences).

**Common mistake**: Spending too long on Attention and skipping Desire. Without proof and vision, interest does not convert to action.

### 4. PAS (Problem -> Agitation -> Solution)

```
Problem    -> Name the specific pain. Be precise, not generic.
             "Your PR review queue is 47 items deep."

Agitation  -> Make the pain vivid. Quantify the cost. Show consequences.
             "That's 47 developers waiting. $23,000/day in blocked
              engineering time. And your best reviewer just gave notice."

Solution   -> Position your product as the relief.
             "CodePilot reviews every PR in under 2 hours, with the same
              contextual understanding as your senior engineers."
```

**When to use**: Cold email openers, blog introductions, social hooks for audiences that do not yet know they have the problem or have not prioritized it (unaware audiences).

**Key difference from AIDA**: PAS starts with pain. AIDA starts with attention (which could be positive). Use PAS when the audience needs to feel the problem before they will consider a solution.

**Common mistake**: Weak agitation. If the agitation step does not make the reader uncomfortable, it is not doing its job. Quantify the cost of inaction.

### 5. Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD)

**Source**: Clayton Christensen, Tony Ulwick, Bob Moesta

```
Framework sentence:
"When I [situation/context],
 I want to [motivation/job],
 so I can [expected outcome]."

Example:
"When I'm scaling my engineering team past 50 developers,
 I want to maintain code quality without bottlenecking on senior reviewers,
 so I can ship features on schedule and keep my best engineers focused
 on architecture instead of line-by-line reviews."
```

**When to use**: Pressure-test every value proposition. If you cannot frame your value as a job the customer is hiring your product to do, the positioning is feature-centric, not outcome-centric.

**Four forces of switching**:

```
Push of current situation   -> What is wrong with the status quo?
Pull of new solution        -> What is attractive about the alternative?
Anxiety of new solution     -> What could go wrong if I switch?
Habit of current situation  -> What keeps me doing what I'm doing?

Your messaging must:
- Amplify push (make status quo pain vivid)
- Amplify pull (make your solution attractive)
- Reduce anxiety (proof points, guarantees, easy onboarding)
- Overcome habit (migration support, backward compatibility)
```

**Common mistake**: Focusing only on pull (how great your product is) without addressing anxiety and habit. Customers do not buy because your product is good. They buy because the pain of staying outweighs the risk of switching.

### 6. Category Design (Play Bigger)

**Source**: "Play Bigger" by Al Ramadan, Dave Peterson, Christopher Lochhead, Kevin Maney

```
When to create a new category:
- Existing categories carry baggage that hurts your positioning
- Your product does something fundamentally different from existing labels
- You are trying to change how people think about a problem, not just
  provide a better solution within the current framing

Category creation requires:
1. Category Name     -> 2-4 words, descriptive, ownable
2. Point of View     -> A strong opinion about why the world needs this category
3. Lightning Strike  -> A moment (event, launch, content) that defines the category
                        in the market's mind

Risks:
- Category creation is expensive (you must educate the market)
- It fails if you cannot sustain messaging for 18-24 months
- It fails if the category name does not "click" intuitively
```

**When to use**: Tier 1 launch plans where the company is redefining a market. Positioning documents where existing categories are limiting. Analyst briefs where you need to propose a new evaluation framework.

**Common mistake**: Creating a category when you should be competing in an existing one. Category creation is not a shortcut to avoid competition. It is a strategic choice that requires sustained investment.

### 7. Hook Model (Nir Eyal)

**Source**: "Hooked" by Nir Eyal

```
Trigger     -> External (email, notification, ad) or Internal (emotion, habit)
Action      -> The simplest behavior in anticipation of reward
Variable Reward -> Unpredictable positive outcome (social, hunt, self)
Investment  -> User puts something in (data, content, social capital)
             that makes the next trigger more likely
```

**When to use**: Nurture sequences (map each email to a hook cycle stage), PLG onboarding flows, retention campaigns, community engagement loops.

**Mapping to email sequences**:

```
Email 1 (Trigger):    External trigger -- "You created a project but haven't
                      invited your team. Here's why teams get 3x more value."
Email 2 (Action):     Make the next step trivially easy -- "Click here to
                      invite your first teammate. Takes 10 seconds."
Email 3 (Reward):     Show variable reward -- "Teams like yours saw a 40%
                      productivity boost. Here's what they discovered."
Email 4 (Investment): Ask for investment -- "Set up your first workflow
                      template. It gets smarter the more you use it."
Email 5 (Trigger):    Create internal trigger -- "Your trial ends in 3 days.
                      Your team has already saved 12 hours this week."
```

### 8. Pirate Metrics / AARRR (Dave McClure)

**Source**: Dave McClure, 500 Startups

```
Acquisition  -> How do users find you?
               Metrics: traffic, signups, cost per acquisition
Activation   -> Do users have a good first experience?
               Metrics: onboarding completion, time-to-value, "aha moment" rate
Retention    -> Do users come back?
               Metrics: DAU/MAU, churn rate, session frequency
Revenue      -> Do users pay?
               Metrics: conversion rate, ARPU, LTV
Referral     -> Do users tell others?
               Metrics: NPS, viral coefficient, referral rate
```

**When to use**: Before every campaign brief, diagnose which stage is the bottleneck. The most common marketing mistake is running acquisition campaigns when the real problem is activation or retention.

**Diagnostic questions**:

```
If traffic is high but signups are low        -> Acquisition problem
If signups are high but activation is low     -> Activation problem
If activation is high but users churn         -> Retention problem
If retention is good but revenue is low       -> Monetization problem
If everything works but growth is slow        -> Referral problem
```

**Rule**: Fix the bottom of the funnel before the top. Pouring more users into a leaky funnel is expensive and wasteful.

### 9. Content-Market Fit

```
Content-Market Fit exists at the intersection of:
1. What you can credibly say (you have proof, expertise, or data)
2. What your audience needs to hear (they have a question, pain, or aspiration)
3. What no one else is saying (or saying well)

Test every content idea against all three:
- Can we credibly say this? (Do we have proof?)
- Does our audience need to hear this? (Is there search volume, social signal?)
- Is anyone else saying this well? (If yes, can we add something unique?)

If any answer is "no," kill the idea or strengthen the weak dimension.
```

**When to use**: Content pack creation, editorial calendar planning, blog topic selection, community content pillars.

**Common mistake**: Creating content that passes tests 1 and 2 but fails test 3. "10 Best Practices for Code Review" is credible and needed, but 500 other articles already cover it. Find the angle that makes yours the only one worth reading.

### 10. Message-Market Fit Testing

```
Before scaling any message to paid channels:

1. Define 3+ message variants
   - Vary the value proposition angle (not just the words)
   - Example: outcome-led vs. pain-led vs. social-proof-led

2. Set minimum sample size
   - Paid ads: 5,000 impressions per variant minimum
   - Email: 1,000 recipients per variant minimum
   - Landing pages: 500 visits per variant minimum

3. Define success metric and significance threshold
   - CTR, conversion rate, or pipeline influenced
   - p < 0.05 or practical significance (>20% relative lift)

4. Run test for minimum duration
   - Paid ads: 7 days minimum (captures day-of-week variance)
   - Email: 48 hours minimum (captures timezone variance)

5. Decision rule
   - Winner by significance -> scale winner
   - No clear winner -> both messages are weak, develop new variants
   - Winner but small lift -> test a more radical variant next
```

**When to use**: Any campaign brief that includes paid spend. Include the test plan in the hypotheses section.

### 11. Full-Funnel Attribution

```
Attribution models, in order of sophistication:

Last-touch:      Gives 100% credit to the last touchpoint before conversion.
                 Simple but misleading. Ignores everything that built awareness.

First-touch:     Gives 100% credit to the first touchpoint.
                 Values discovery but ignores nurture.

Linear:          Splits credit equally across all touchpoints.
                 Fair but uninformative -- no signal about what actually matters.

Time-decay:      More credit to recent touchpoints.
                 Better than linear. Biased toward bottom-of-funnel.

Position-based:  40% first, 40% last, 20% split across middle.
                 Good default for most B2B campaigns.

Algorithmic:     ML-weighted based on historical conversion patterns.
                 Best accuracy. Requires volume and tooling.
```

**When to use**: Defining success metrics in any campaign brief. Always state which model you are using and why. Never report results without specifying the attribution model.

**Rule**: The attribution model you choose will determine what looks successful. Choose the model before the campaign launches, not after.

### 12. Brand Narrative Arc

```
Origin     -> Why this company exists. What problem the founders saw.
              "We were a team of 8 engineers at a Series B startup.
               Our best reviewer spent half her week reading other
               people's code instead of building the architecture
               we hired her for."

Tension    -> The conflict that makes the story worth telling.
              "Every AI coding tool was focused on writing more code
               faster. Nobody was solving the review bottleneck that
               was actually slowing teams down."

Resolution -> How the product resolves the tension.
              "We built an AI that learns how your team writes code
               and reviews every PR with the same contextual understanding
               as your most experienced engineer."

Vision     -> Where the story goes from here.
              "We believe every engineering team deserves a world where
               code quality and shipping speed are not in tension.
               That's the future we're building."
```

**When to use**: About pages, founder storytelling, brand manifestos, investor presentations, Tier 1 launch narratives.

**Key rule**: The tension is what makes the story worth telling. Without tension, there is no story -- just a feature list. The tension must be real and specific, not manufactured.

## Quick Reference: Heuristic Responses

When you encounter these patterns in a request, apply these responses immediately:

| You Hear | You Think | You Do |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| "Increase awareness" | Wrong bottleneck | Diagnose full AARRR funnel first |
| "Make it go viral" | Not a strategy | Define distribution mechanics |
| "Our product is for everyone" | Product is for no one | Force ICP specificity |
| "We need more content" | Random acts of marketing | Build narrative pillars first |
| "Just copy what [competitor] does" | Playing their game | Reframe to your strengths |
| "Let's A/B test the button color" | Optimizing the wrong thing | Test the message, not the color |
| "What's our ROI on content?" | Wrong attribution model | Define the model before measuring |
| "We need a rebrand" | Probably a positioning problem | Diagnose positioning first |
| "Let's do a webinar" | Tactic without strategy | Define the funnel stage and audience first |
| "Can we add more logos?" | Social proof as wallpaper | Curate 3-5 logos that match the ICP |
