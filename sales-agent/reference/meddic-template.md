## Metrics | Score: _/5

**Definition**: The quantified business outcomes the buyer expects from solving this problem. Not features, not vague improvements. Hard numbers tied to KPIs the buyer already tracks.

**What "good" looks like**: The buyer can state, in their own words, the dollar value or measurable improvement they expect, and the timeline for achieving it.

### Evidence

*What has the buyer told you about their expected outcomes? Include specific quotes, data points, and the source (which call, which stakeholder).*

### Gaps

*What do you still not know? What assumptions are you making?*

### Coaching Questions

Ask these to pressure-test the score:

- "What specific metric does the buyer expect to improve, and by how much?"
- "Did the buyer provide this metric, or did you suggest it?"
- "Can you tie this metric to a dollar value? If not, is it really a decision driver?"
- "Has the buyer shared this metric with their leadership? Is it part of their goals?"
- "If we delivered exactly this metric, would they renew? If not, what else matters?"

### Scoring Guide

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | No metrics identified. Buyer has not articulated expected outcomes. |
| 2 | Vague metrics ("improve productivity"). Not quantified. |
| 3 | Specific metric identified ("reduce cycle time by 30%") but not validated by buyer or tied to dollars. |
| 4 | Quantified metric tied to business value ("$2.4M/year in engineering time"), stated by the buyer. |
| 5 | Quantified metric agreed upon as success criteria, with baseline measurement and timeline for evaluation. |

## Economic Buyer | Score: _/5

**Definition**: The person with the authority to approve the budget and veto the deal. Not the champion, not the evaluator, not the person who wants it. The person who can write the check.

**What "good" looks like**: You have met the economic buyer, understand their priorities, and they have confirmed that this initiative is a priority with allocated budget.

### Evidence

*Who is the economic buyer? Have you met them? What do they care about? What did they say?*

### Gaps

*Have you validated this person actually has authority? Have you met them directly?*

### Coaching Questions

- "Have you personally met the economic buyer, or is this based on what your champion told you?"
- "What are the economic buyer's top 3 priorities this quarter? Where does your deal rank?"
- "If the economic buyer had to choose between your deal and another initiative for the same budget, what would happen?"
- "Can the economic buyer unilaterally approve, or do they need board/finance committee sign-off?"
- "What is the economic buyer's relationship with your competitor, if any?"

### Scoring Guide

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Economic buyer not identified. Do not know who holds budget authority. |
| 2 | Economic buyer identified by name/title but no direct engagement. Relying on champion's description. |
| 3 | Met the economic buyer once. Understand their role. Have not discussed priorities or budget. |
| 4 | Met the economic buyer. They have confirmed the initiative is a priority and budget exists. |
| 5 | Regular engagement with economic buyer. They are actively sponsoring the deal and have confirmed budget, timeline, and success criteria. |

## Decision Criteria | Score: _/5

**Definition**: The formal and informal criteria the buyer will use to make their decision. Includes technical requirements, business outcomes, risk factors, and political considerations.

**What "good" looks like**: You have the evaluation criteria in writing, you can map your solution to each criterion with evidence, and you know which criteria favor you and which favor the competitor.

### Evidence

*What are the stated criteria? Where did you get them? Are there unstated criteria?*

### Gaps

*Which criteria do you not fully understand? Which might favor the competitor?*

### Coaching Questions

- "Can you list the top 5 decision criteria in order of importance? Where did this ranking come from?"
- "Which criteria can only we satisfy? Which can only the competitor satisfy?"
- "Are there unstated criteria (e.g., existing relationships, risk aversion, vendor size) that could override the formal evaluation?"
- "If you scored yourself against each criterion, where are you weakest?"
- "Has the buyer weighted the criteria, or are they treating everything as equally important?"

### Scoring Guide

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Decision criteria unknown. Guessing what matters. |
| 2 | General understanding of what matters ("they care about ease of use") but no formal criteria. |
| 3 | Have the formal evaluation criteria. Can map our solution to most of them. |
| 4 | Have the criteria with weighting/ranking. Can map our solution with evidence for each. Know where competitor is strong. |
| 5 | Helped shape the criteria. Our differentiators are embedded in the evaluation framework. Competitor will struggle to meet key criteria. |

## Decision Process | Score: _/5

**Definition**: The literal steps from "we want to buy" to "contract signed." Every step, every approver, every timeline. Includes procurement, legal, security, and any governance gates.

**What "good" looks like**: You can draw the process on a whiteboard with dates, and the buyer has validated it.

### Evidence

*What is the process? Who are the approvers? What are the timelines for each step?*

### Gaps

*Which steps are you unsure about? Have you accounted for security review, legal, and procurement?*

### Coaching Questions

- "Walk me through every step from 'team recommends us' to 'contract signed.' How many weeks does each step take?"
- "Who else needs to approve besides the economic buyer? (Legal, security, procurement, board?)"
- "Have you asked the buyer to validate your understanding of the process?"
- "What is the longest any of these steps has taken for a deal of this size at this company?"
- "Is your close date based on the buyer's process timeline, or on your wishful thinking?"

### Scoring Guide

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Process unknown. Operating on assumptions about how the deal will close. |
| 2 | High-level understanding ("they'll evaluate, then buy"). No step-by-step process mapped. |
| 3 | Have the major steps identified but missing timelines or minor steps (security, legal). |
| 4 | Full process mapped with steps, approvers, and estimated timelines. Buyer has validated. |
| 5 | Full process mapped, validated, and we have proactively started downstream steps (sent security docs, engaged procurement). Next steps are on the calendar. |

## Identify Pain | Score: _/5

**Definition**: The business pain driving the initiative. Must be quantified, felt by the buyer, and tied to a metric that matters to leadership. Surface-level pain ("we need better tools") is not sufficient.

**What "good" looks like**: The buyer can articulate the pain in their own words, quantify its cost, and explain why solving it is urgent.

### Evidence

*What is the pain? Who articulated it? Is it quantified? Is it urgent?*

### Gaps

*Is the pain felt at the leadership level? Is there a compelling event creating urgency?*

### Coaching Questions

- "Can you state the buyer's pain in one sentence, in their words?"
- "What is the dollar cost of this pain per quarter? Did the buyer provide this number or did you calculate it?"
- "What happens if they do nothing for 12 months? Is the pain getting worse?"
- "Is this pain on the economic buyer's radar, or only felt at the operational level?"
- "What is the compelling event? Why must this be solved now and not next year?"

### Scoring Guide

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | No pain identified. The buyer is exploring, not buying. |
| 2 | Pain acknowledged but vague ("we need to improve"). Not quantified. |
| 3 | Pain is specific and articulated by the buyer ("cycle time is 2x what it should be"). Not yet quantified in dollars. |
| 4 | Pain is quantified ("$2.4M/year in lost productivity"), acknowledged by leadership, and tied to a priority. |
| 5 | Pain is quantified, urgent (compelling event), felt at the economic buyer level, and getting worse over time. Cost of inaction is clear. |

## Champion | Score: _/5

**Definition**: An internal advocate who has (1) organizational power or influence, (2) a personal reason to see you win, and (3) the ability and willingness to sell for you when you are not in the room.

**What "good" looks like**: Your champion proactively shares information, sets up meetings, pushes the deal forward internally, and can articulate your value to other stakeholders.

### Evidence

*Who is the champion? What have they done to advance the deal? What is their personal motivation?*

### Gaps

*Have you tested whether they can sell internally? Do they have real influence or just enthusiasm?*

### Champion Litmus Test

Your contact is a champion only if they pass ALL of these tests:

- [ ] **Power**: They have organizational influence (title, tenure, relationships) that matters in the decision.
- [ ] **Access**: They can get you meetings with the economic buyer and other stakeholders.
- [ ] **Motivation**: They have a personal win if you succeed (promotion, recognition, solving their problem).
- [ ] **Articulation**: They can explain your value proposition to another stakeholder without your help.
- [ ] **Action**: They have taken concrete actions to advance the deal (not just been friendly and responsive).

If your contact fails 2+ of these tests, they are a contact or a coach, not a champion.

### Coaching Questions

- "What has your champion done to advance the deal that they did not have to do?"
- "If your champion left the company tomorrow, what happens to the deal?"
- "Can your champion articulate your value proposition without your help? Have you tested this?"
- "What does your champion personally gain if this deal closes?"
- "Have you armed your champion with the materials they need to sell internally?"

### Scoring Guide

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | No champion. Operating without an internal advocate. |
| 2 | Friendly contact who likes you but has not taken action to advance the deal or lacks influence. |
| 3 | Supportive stakeholder with some influence. Has shared information and been responsive. Has not actively sold internally. |
| 4 | Active champion with influence. Has set up key meetings, shared internal information, and articulated your value to others. |
| 5 | Powerful champion who is actively sponsoring the deal. Has influence with the economic buyer, personal motivation to see you win, and has demonstrated internal selling. |

## Paper Process (MEDDPICC Extension) | Score: _/5

*Use for deals over $200K ACV or at companies with known procurement complexity.*

**Definition**: The legal, procurement, and security review steps required to get from "verbal yes" to "signed contract." This is where most "sure thing" deals die.

### Evidence

*What is the procurement process? Security review requirements? Legal review timeline?*

### Coaching Questions

- "How long did their last vendor contract of this size take from verbal to signed?"
- "Have you sent your security documentation proactively?"
- "Who in procurement will handle this? Have you engaged them?"
- "Are there any terms that are absolute deal-breakers for their legal team?"
- "Is there a fiscal year deadline that creates urgency for procurement?"

### Scoring Guide

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Paper process unknown. Have not discussed procurement, legal, or security. |
| 2 | Aware that review is required but no details on steps, timeline, or contacts. |
| 3 | Know the major steps. Have not engaged procurement or legal yet. |
| 4 | Steps mapped, contacts identified, security docs sent proactively. |
| 5 | Procurement and legal engaged, timeline agreed, no blocking issues identified. |

## Implications of Pain (MEDDPICC Extension) | Score: _/5

*Use for deals over $200K ACV to strengthen the business case.*

**Definition**: The compounding cost of doing nothing. What happens if the buyer does not solve this problem? How does it get worse over time?

### Evidence

*What is the cost of inaction? How does the pain compound?*

### Coaching Questions

- "If the buyer does nothing for 12 months, what is the total cost?"
- "Is the pain getting worse, stable, or improving on its own?"
- "Can you tie the cost of inaction to a metric the economic buyer tracks?"
- "Has the buyer acknowledged the cost of inaction, or are you assuming it?"

### Scoring Guide

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Cost of inaction not discussed. No urgency. |
| 2 | General sense that "things will get worse" but not quantified. |
| 3 | Buyer acknowledges the problem will persist but has not quantified the compounding cost. |
| 4 | Cost of inaction is quantified and acknowledged by the buyer. |
| 5 | Cost of inaction is quantified, compounding, and presented to the economic buyer as part of the business case. |

## Composite Score and Risk Assessment

| Total Score | Risk Level | Guidance |
|-------------|-----------|----------|
| 25-30 (MEDDIC) / 35-40 (MEDDPICC) | Low | Forecast as Commit if close date is within cycle |
| 18-24 / 25-34 | Medium | Forecast as Best Case. Address gaps before next review. |
| 12-17 / 17-24 | High | Forecast as Pipeline. Do not commit resources until gaps are closed. |
| Below 12 / Below 17 | Critical | Qualification in question. Review whether to continue investing. |

---

## After Scoring: Required Actions

1. For every element scored 1-2: create a specific action with owner and due date to close the gap.
2. For every element scored 3: identify what evidence would move it to 4 and plan to gather it.
3. Review scores weekly. Any element that has not improved in 2 weeks = escalate.
4. Share the scorecard with your manager before every deal review.
5. Record outcomes (won/lost) with updated scores for the flywheel learning loop.
